We have presented a simple and cheap solution to help reduce food-waste, with a potential side-effect of helping the poor. In an ideal world, that would be more than enough to have companies jump all over it. However, we have to be realistic. We cannot rely on the benevolence of companies in todays society. Bottom line, it is all about money.

The problem of money

Unfortunately, saving the environment is rarely profitable. The biggest roadblock on the way to a sustainable environment is money, and unless science can come up with a solution, it will continue to be that way. But let us avoid turning this into an environmental debate, and rather look at how this problem extends to FoodHut.

It always comes down to profit - and it will be no different in this case. There is no real incentive for companies, economically, to implement this feature. While it does not demand a lot of resources, nor a lot of work, it will not make them money. There is also the argument that it might lead to people buying less, further reducing profit. While this creates economical incentive to reduce food-waste, it is still "bad for business".

The solution

This leaves us with few options. We either create incentive, or it needs to be required by law.

Legislation is the simplest, and probably best option, as it would ensure that it is implemented everywhere. This would be the preferred solution for the future, but it might take time and it can be difficult to get through.

There is however a third option, a middle ground between the first two, that could be easier to realize.

Using Norway as an example, we have something called "Svanemerket", which could be translated to something like the "Swan badge". This is a badge put on products fulfilling certain environmental-friendly requirements. Grocery stores and supermarkets might also be branded with the Swan provided they meet the criteria, one of which focuses on minimizing food-waste. Adding the implementation of FoodHut as a criteria would help create incentive.

You can read more about "Svanemerket" for grocery stores here.

The problem with this solution is that, as it is now, the incentive it creates might not be enough. You get to advertise that your business is eco-friendly, and that's about it. For this solution to be acceptable, there needs to be certain boons tied to achieving the brand, likely in the form of government aid of some kind.

Unfortunately, all this does is move the problem of incentive from private companies to the government. On the upside, governments in general should be more interested in being eco-friendly, in effort to reach set goals and the goals of international treaties.

In any case, the government needs to be involved.
